About Our Solar System

Our solar system comprises the Sun and all celestial bodies bound to it by gravity. This includes eight planets, five officially recognized dwarf planets, over 300 moons, more than 1.3 million asteroids, and approximately 3,900 comets. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

The Sun, a G2V main-sequence star, contains 99.86% of the solar system's mass. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9} The planets, in order from the Sun, are Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. The dwarf planets include Pluto, Eris, Haumea, Makemake, and Ceres.

The solar system is located in the Orion Spur of the Milky Way galaxy and takes about 230 million years to orbit the galactic center. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Beyond Neptune lies the Kuiper Belt, a region filled with icy bodies and dwarf planets. Further out is the hypothesized Oort Cloud, believed to be the source of long-period comets. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Meteoroids, which are smaller than asteroids, travel through space and can enter Earth's atmosphere, becoming meteors. If they survive the journey and land on Earth, they're called meteorites. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}
